# Citation-Graph Traversal (Snowballing) vs Keyword Search: Recall/Precision and the Task-Conditioned Lesson for the Librarian

**Sources:** Jalali & Wohlin, "Systematic Literature Studies: Database Searches vs. Backward Snowballing" (ESEM 2012, wohlin.eu/esem12a.pdf); Wohlin, "Guidelines for Snowballing in Systematic Literature Studies and a Replication in SE" (EASE 2014, wohlin.eu/ease14.pdf); Felizardo, Mendes, Kalinowski et al., "Using Forward Snowballing to Update SLRs in SE" (ESEM 2016); Badampudi, Wohlin & Petersen, "Experiences from Using Snowballing and Database Searches" (EASE 2015); Mourão, Pimentel, Murta, Kalinowski, Mendes & Wohlin, "On the Performance of Hybrid Search Strategies for SLRs in SE" (*IST* 2020, arXiv:2004.09741) and "Investigating the Use of a Hybrid Search Strategy" (ESEM 2017, wohlin.eu/esem17.pdf); Haddaway et al., "citationchaser" (*Research Synthesis Methods* 2022, doi:10.1002/jrsm.1563); "From Little Things Big Things Grow: Seed Studies for Medical SR Literature Search" (arXiv:2204.03096); Cochrane Handbook (reference-list checking is mandatory). RAG/agentic analogues: Stop-RAG (arXiv:2510.14337), PAR2-RAG (arXiv:2603.29085), AIR (arXiv:2005.01218), RT-RAG (arXiv:2601.11255).

## 1. The two strategies and the core trade-off

Keyword/database search (DBS) issues a Boolean/lexical query against an index; **snowballing** (a.k.a. citation chasing, pearl-growing, footnote-chasing) starts from a *seed/start set* and walks the citation graph — *backward* (papers the seed cites) and *forward* (papers that cite the seed), iterating until no new relevant items appear (Wohlin 2014). The consistent empirical finding across a decade of SE work is a **precision–recall split**: snowballing — especially **forward** snowballing — yields **higher precision** (less noise to screen) at **slightly lower recall**, while DBS yields **higher recall** but far more noise. Jalali & Wohlin (2012) ran the same SLR both ways and found *largely the same included set and identical conclusions*, but DBS carried **~85% noise vs ~32%** for snowballing. Felizardo et al. (2016), updating an SLR by forward snowballing, **cut the studies to screen by >5×** while **missing only one relevant study** — a clean illustration that the graph route trades a small recall hit for a large precision/effort win.

## 2. How much does the graph find that keywords miss — and vice versa

The numbers cut both ways and are the heart of the task-conditioned lesson. **Snowballing recovers a large slice keyword search misses:** up to **51% of included studies** in a review have been attributed to snowballing, and Boolean queries alone failed to reach total recall on several topics where snowballing closed the gap (arXiv:2204.03096). In medicine, only **~75.5% of included studies** were retrievable from a single database (PubMed) — the missing ~24.5% came via other databases or citation chains; Cochrane therefore makes reference-list checking **mandatory**. **But keyword search has the higher ceiling on recall**, and snowballing's recall is *entirely hostage to the seed set*: a single representative library (Scopus) found only **13–35%** of relevant papers alone, Scopus+ACM **23–60%** (Mourão et al. 2020). The reconciliation everyone converges on is **hybrid**: a representative digital library *plus* parallel/sequential snowballing gives the best precision/recall balance — though on **broad populations** (beyond a tight SE topic) the hybrid's *relative recall can drop*, i.e. graph traversal stays incomplete when the field is diffuse.

## 3. Seed quality is the dominant variable — and the closest analogue to our top-k

Wohlin (2014) is explicit: snowballing reliability is "highly dependent on the creation of a suitable start set." A good start set must (a) draw from **different communities, publishers, years, and authors**, (b) be **not too small** (size scales with topic breadth), and (c) come from research-question keywords. The dominant failure mode is **author/cluster overrepresentation** — the graph loops back into one tightly-citing clique and misses sibling clusters, producing *confident but biased* coverage. Wohlin recommends Google Scholar for the seed precisely to avoid *publisher bias*. The transfer to the librarian is exact: **our top-k retrieval is the "seed set," and breadcrumb/neighbour expansion is the "snowball step."** A keyword/embedding query that returns a homogeneous top-k (one book, one author, adjacent chunks) is the RAG version of a clustered start set — high apparent confidence, blind to other clusters.

## 4. Mapping to the librarian: we have breadcrumbs, not a true citation graph

This is the load-bearing caveat. The librarian's chunks carry **breadcrumbs** (document/section structure), not inter-document *citations*. So the snowballing literature transfers as an **analogy with a known weakness**: breadcrumb traversal gives cheap *intra-document* depth ("read the rest of this section/chapter") — the legitimate, high-precision move for **maths/API/single-fact lookup**, where the answer lives in one neighbourhood (cf. Marchionini "lookup," AIR's "stop when no new query terms appear"). It does **not** give *inter-cluster* breadth, which is exactly what real snowballing buys for **literature synthesis**. The corrective for synthesis tasks is to manufacture the missing graph edges with **embedding-space diversity**: deliberately re-query with *paraphrased/orthogonal* terms to jump clusters (the berrypicking / "different communities" move), since the embedding index has no `cited_by` field to walk. The agentic RAG literature has independently rediscovered this: PAR2-RAG "retrieve broadly before committing narrowly," RT-RAG decomposing into reasoning trees to avoid single-path drift, and the iterative-snowball stopping rule (AIR: stop when an iteration surfaces no new terms; Stop-RAG: learn the stop as an MDP) — all are snowballing iteration-and-saturation logic re-cast for LLM retrieval.

## 5. The task-conditioned strategy (the actual answer to this round's question)

- **Lookup (maths / API / single science fact):** the *seed alone* is highest-precision; snowballing seed studies "increases recall but dramatically lowers precision" (arXiv:2204.03096). One query, narrow k, breadcrumb-depth only if the first hit is partial. Do **not** multi-search.
- **Literature synthesis / investigation:** snowballing is *essential* and contributes up to ~51% of the corpus; run **iterated, cluster-jumping re-queries** with diverse terms, stop on **saturation** (no new clusters), and explicitly track source diversity to defeat the overrepresentation bias.
- **Writing / design grounding:** behaves like a *bounded* synthesis — a representative seed plus one snowball pass (hybrid), stopping on coverage-sufficiency rather than total recall.

## 6. Actionable implications for task-conditioned librarian experiments

1. **Treat top-k as a seed set and measure cluster diversity, not just relevance.** Add a metric for *distinct source/author/book clusters* per result set; test the hypothesis that homogeneous seeds (one cluster) under-recall on synthesis exactly as clustered start sets do in Wohlin's data.
2. **Build a "breadcrumb-snowball" expansion mode and A/B it by task.** For synthesis, after the seed query, auto-issue paraphrased/orthogonal re-queries (embedding-space substitute for forward/backward citation edges) and iterate to *saturation*; for lookup, disable it. Predicted result: precision-neutral recall gain on synthesis, pure overhead on lookup (the FLARE "no benefit on short text" pattern).
3. **Adopt a saturation stopping rule for synthesis (AIR-style).** Stop iterating when a new query surfaces no new distinct cluster — the RAG form of "no new papers this snowball round." Compare against fixed-budget and the Round-1 ~1.26-search baseline.
4. **Quantify the recall ceiling per task with a hybrid baseline.** Replicate the Scopus-alone (13–35%) vs Scopus+ACM (23–60%) experiment on the librarian: single-query recall vs single-query + breadcrumb + diverse re-query, on a hand-labelled synthesis gold set. Expect single-query to leave a measurable gap (the ~24.5% "missed by one database" analogue).
5. **Report precision and recall separately, conditioned on task.** Keep the abstention contract (hallucination 0%) fixed; the new axis is *recall without re-introducing unsupported claims* — and snowballing's lesson is that recall gains cost precision, so screening effort (chunks read per answer) must be tracked alongside.
6. **Guard the overrepresentation failure mode.** Encode a *source-diversity floor* (≥ M distinct books/papers) as a complementary stop/continue signal for synthesis, mirroring Wohlin's "different communities/publishers" requirement; flag single-cluster convergence as a confidence downgrade.
