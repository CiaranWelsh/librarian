# Mixed-initiative research: which subtasks humans vs AI do best, handoff patterns, and measured human+AI vs either-alone quality

**Sources:** Vaccaro, Almaatouq & Malone, "When combinations of humans and AI are useful: a systematic review and meta-analysis" (*Nature Human Behaviour* 8(12):2293-2303, 2024; arXiv:2405.06087; 106 experiments / 370 effect sizes / 74 papers, 2020-H1 2023); Bansal et al., "Is the Best Better? (CHI 2021); Hemmer et al., "Complementarity in human-AI collaboration: concept, sources, and evidence" (*EJIS*, 2025; tandfonline 10.1080/0960085X.2025.2475962; arXiv:2404.00029); Horvitz, "Principles of Mixed-Initiative User Interfaces" (CHI'99, pp.159-166); Horvitz & Paek, "Complementary Computing: Policies for Transferring Callers..." (*UMUAI* 17, 2007); Buçinca, Malaya & Gajos, "To Trust or to Think: Cognitive Forcing Functions Can Reduce Overreliance on AI" (*PACMHCI* 5 CSCW1:188, 2021; arXiv:2102.09692); Vasconcelos et al., "Explanations Can Reduce Overreliance on AI Systems During Decision-Making" (*PACMHCI* 2023); Inkpen et al., "Advancing Human-AI Complementarity: ... User Expertise and Algorithmic Tuning" (*ACM ToCHI*, 2022; arXiv:2208.07960); PNAS Nexus complementarity-framework (2026, PMC12983458).

## 1. The blunt headline: naive human+AI usually *loses*, with two clean exceptions

Vaccaro et al.'s meta-analysis is the keystone number. Pooled across 370 effect sizes, the human-AI *combination* performed **significantly worse than the better of human-alone or AI-alone**: synergy **g = -0.23 (95% CI -0.39 to -0.07)**. Two moderators flip the sign and are directly load-bearing for us. **(a) Task type:** decision-making tasks (≈85% of effects) lost (**g = -0.27, CI -0.44 to -0.10**), whereas **content-creation** tasks gained (**g = +0.19, CI -0.09 to +0.48**, n.s. but positive). **(b) Relative baseline:** when the *human* was the stronger solo performer, the team gained (**g = +0.46, CI +0.28 to +0.66**); when the *AI* was stronger, the team lost badly (**g = -0.54, CI -0.71 to -0.37**). The canonical positive example is bird/image classification — **AI 73%, human 81%, team 90%** — synergy precisely because the human started ahead. The lesson is not "collaboration helps" but "collaboration helps **when each party owns the subtask it is better at, and hurts when the weaker party overrides the stronger.**"

## 2. The librarian case is the *favourable* corner, not the failing one

Almost all of §1's negative evidence is **AI-advises-human classification**: AI emits a label/confidence, the human accepts or rejects. The librarian inverts this. Here the *human directs and verifies* while *Claude drives the tool* (query formulation, k-selection, chunk reading, quote extraction, synthesis). That is structurally a **content-creation / synthesis task with the human as the stronger solo performer on judgement and the AI as the stronger performer on retrieval throughput** — exactly the two corners where the meta-analysis shows *gains* (g = +0.19 and +0.46). Bansal et al.'s CHI'21 "complementary team performance" framing makes the requirement explicit: synergy needs **genuine capability asymmetry plus a mechanism that routes each subtask to its better owner.** Hemmer et al. (EJIS 2025) formalise the two sources of that asymmetry — **information asymmetry** (each party sees evidence the other doesn't) and **capability asymmetry** (each party is better at a different operation). The librarian supplies both: the corpus holds information neither party memorised; Claude is faster at high-recall search and quote-grounding, the human is better at framing the question, judging relevance, and catching subtle wrongness.

## 3. The handoff is the actual design object (Horvitz, mixed-initiative)

Horvitz's CHI'99 principles are the design grammar for *who acts when*: an agent should add value through automation, **maintain a representation of uncertainty**, only take initiative when expected value justifies it, and above all **"provide mechanisms for efficient agent-user collaboration to refine results"** and minimise the cost of the human correcting the agent. His later "complementary computing" work (Horvitz & Paek 2007) is literally a **transfer-of-control policy** — keep the automated path while expected utility is high, hand off to the human at the decision boundary. Mapped to the librarian, the handoff points are: (i) **before search** — does Claude answer from parametric memory or query the corpus (the abstention/route decision, settled separately)?; (ii) **at low confidence** — the librarian's confidence label is the natural transfer-of-control trigger to surface uncertainty to the human rather than fabricate; (iii) **at synthesis** — Claude drafts grounded prose, the human adjudicates contested or thin claims. The 2026 PNAS Nexus framework names the same primitives: *partition roles, orchestrate attention and interrogation, build a knowledge infrastructure.* The librarian **is** the knowledge infrastructure; the open question is the attention/interrogation policy.

## 4. The dominant failure mode is overreliance — and the fix is friction, not explanation

Across decision studies, **overreliance** (accepting wrong AI output without checking) is the most frequent error and the main driver of §1's negative g. Crucially for our design, in Vaccaro et al. **explanations, confidence displays, participant type, and division-of-labour were all non-significant moderators** of synergy — i.e. *showing* more does not fix it. What does: **cognitive forcing functions** (Buçinca et al. 2021) — making the human commit/think before the AI answer is revealed measurably raised error-detection on wrong AI suggestions (**0.03 → 0.09, p = .003**) and cut overreliance, though high-Need-for-Cognition users *disliked* the friction (a trust-vs-performance trade-off). Vasconcelos et al. (2023) refine this: explanations reduce overreliance **only when engaging with the explanation is cheaper than verifying independently** — overreliance is a rational cost-benefit response, not pure laziness. For the librarian this argues for **quote-first grounding plus an explicit verify step as the forcing function**: surfacing the source chunk lowers the human's verification cost (so deliberation becomes worthwhile) without relying on a free-text "explanation" the human will rubber-stamp.

## 5. Actionable implications for task-conditioned librarian experiments

1. **Adopt synergy/augmentation as the headline metric, not raw accuracy.** Report Vaccaro et al.'s two quantities: **augmentation** (team vs human-alone — does the librarian help the engineer?) and **synergy** (team vs *best* of either alone — does it beat Claude-alone-from-memory *and* the human-alone-with-grep?). Pre-register the human-alone and AI-alone arms so synergy is computable; without the both-alone baselines we cannot claim complementarity.

2. **Route by task type, predicting sign from §1.** Synthesis/writing tasks sit in the positive (content-creation) corner; lookup/maths/coding sit where AI-alone is often the stronger solo performer — exactly where naive teaming *loses* (g = -0.54). Hypothesis: the librarian helps most on **literature-synthesis and unfamiliar-domain learning**, and risks *hurting* on tasks Claude already nails from memory (the abstention/no-retrieve route should win there). Test whether forcing retrieval on easy tasks degrades quality.

3. **Instrument the confidence label as a transfer-of-control trigger (Horvitz/Paek).** Treat low-confidence retrievals as a *handoff event*: measure whether escalating to the human (surface uncertainty + ask) beats both silent abstention and confident continuation. This is the librarian's "complementary computing policy."

4. **Add a cognitive-forcing variant and measure overreliance directly.** Compare (a) librarian answers with quote-first grounding, (b) the same plus a forced "which claims are weakly supported?" self-check before final synthesis. Outcome metric = error-detection on *seeded wrong/absent evidence* (à la Buçinca's 0.03→0.09), not user-reported trust — and watch for the trust-vs-performance trade-off in expert users.

5. **Manipulate division of labour explicitly even though it was n.s. in aggregate.** The meta-analysis found no *average* division-of-labour effect, but its studies were AI-advises-human; the librarian's human-directs-AI inversion is undertested. Compare AI-full-task (Claude searches, reads, writes unsupervised) vs AI-subtask + human-adjudication at the handoff points in §3. The PNAS Nexus prediction is that **AI-owns-subtasks beats AI-owns-whole-task**; verify on our corpus.

6. **Control for verification cost (Vasconcelos).** Overreliance is rational when checking is expensive. Vary how cheaply the human can verify a librarian claim — breadcrumb-to-source one click vs. raw chunk vs. no source — and measure whether cheaper verification actually shifts behaviour from acceptance to checking, closing the overreliance gap.
