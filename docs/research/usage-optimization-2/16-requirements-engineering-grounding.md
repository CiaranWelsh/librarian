# Requirements-Engineering Grounding: How Practitioners Consult Method Books Mid-Task

**Round-2 question:** Round 1 fixed the *single-query* mechanics of the librarian (verbatim query, k=20/8, quote-first, abstention). This note asks the *task-conditioned* question for one task family — **requirements work** — by asking how human analysts actually consult method books (Volere, SWEBOK, MRP) mid-task, and what that implies for how an AI assistant should drive the librarian during requirements synthesis.

## Key findings

**1. The Volere method is explicitly engineered for section-level, just-in-time lookup — not cover-to-cover reading.** Each template section ships with a fixed five-field gloss — *Content, Motivation, Considerations, Examples, Form* — designed to be consulted at the moment you write that section (MRP 4e, *Mastering the Requirements Process*, EPUB #5056, #5061). The atomic-requirement "snow card" / requirements shell (rationale, fit criterion, source, priority, etc.) is the unit of consultation during elicitation: you pull the *shell definition for one requirement type*, not the chapter (#5234, #3521). The Volere Requirements Knowledge Model (Appendix B/C) is a cross-reference graph from concept → template section (#6562, #5059, #6245). This is structurally a **retrieval index**, which maps cleanly onto top-k chunk retrieval: the natural query unit is "how do I write the fit criterion for a performance requirement," returning a 1-2 chunk answer, not a topic dump.

**2. Fit criteria show the depth/breadth split inside a single task.** MRP states most performance requirements are *already* quantified, so the fit criterion is mechanical and needs no lookup (#5689, #3521); the lookup is needed only for the *unquantified* exception (#3620). Translation for usage strategy: within requirements work, most sub-steps are shallow (1 confirmatory query or none) and a minority are deep (the genuinely ambiguous requirement). A flat "always retrieve N times" policy mis-serves both ends — exactly the always-on RAG failure mode below.

**3. The empirical reality: practitioners consult formal standards rarely, by personal choice, and lean on templates + copy-paste over standards.** Franch, Glinz, Méndez & Seyff (IEEE TSE 2021, arXiv:2105.13961) found **~47%** of working requirements engineers/business analysts **do not know** the core RE standard ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148; among those who do know it, usage is sporadic (only "in some projects or never" for the majority); and crucially, standards are used **"mostly by personal decision rather than imposed by company, customer, or regulator,"** with cultural/organizational barriers to adoption. The 12-company interview studies (Wagner et al., *Req. Eng.* 2020/2023) corroborate: natural language is the main artifact, "templates or guidelines" are common but a *fixed standard is seldom followed*, and **copy-paste reuse beats template reuse**. Implication: a human's mid-task consult is *targeted and exception-driven*, triggered by a specific doubt, not a ritual scan — the assistant should mimic this, retrieving on demand rather than per-section.

**4. The AI/RAG literature has independently converged on task/complexity-conditioned retrieval.** Always-on retrieval injects noise and cost on queries the model could answer alone; **Adaptive-RAG** (Jeong et al., NAACL 2024, arXiv:2403.14403) routes by query complexity into three lanes — no-retrieval / single-step / iterative multi-step — via a small learned classifier, improving both accuracy and efficiency over one-size-fits-all. Self-RAG (Asai et al.) and FLARE/DRAGIN trigger retrieval on generation *uncertainty* (reflection tokens, low-confidence tokens). A 2026 tier-based study (arXiv:2604.14222) finds **no single retrieval paradigm dominates**: vector RAG wins multi-document **synthesis**, hybrid wins cross-reference, tree-reasoning wins multi-hop — empirical support that *synthesis ≠ reasoning ≠ lookup* and each wants a different strategy. LLM-as-classifier routing adds ~200-300ms/query but needs no training.

## Mapping to the librarian: what "requirements work" demands

Requirements synthesis on the librarian is a **breadth-first, citation-heavy, low-ambiguity** task: the answers exist verbatim in MRP/Volere, the failure mode is *miscitation/fabrication of a section*, not reasoning error. This is the opposite profile to a maths or debugging task (deep, iterative, model-reasoning-dominant). Concretely it favours: (a) **one well-aimed verbatim query per requirement-artifact** (fit criterion, snow card field, business-event, product-boundary) rather than broad refinement chains; (b) **higher k for synthesis sub-tasks** that aggregate across template sections (k=20), but **k=8 and stop** for single-definition confirms; (c) **exception-triggered depth** — only the genuinely ambiguous requirement warrants a second, refined query.

## Actionable implications for task-conditioned librarian experiments

1. **Add a task-type router as the primary independent variable.** Test ≥3 lanes — *no-retrieve* (model knows it), *single verbatim query* (confirm one definition), *multi-query breadth* (synthesize across sections) — and measure whether routing beats a fixed k=20-always policy on accuracy *and* query count. This is the Adaptive-RAG hypothesis ported to the librarian.

2. **Use citation-grounding rate, not answer correctness, as the requirements-task metric.** The RE failure mode is fabricated/mis-attributed template sections (Franch et al. show even humans under-use the real standard). Score every claim against a returned `source_id`; reuse the Round-1 abstention contract as the floor.

3. **Make the stopping rule exception-driven, mirroring the fit-criterion pattern.** Default to *one* query per atomic-requirement field; escalate to a second refined query *only* when the first batch's confidence label is low or chunks conflict. Measure wasted-query rate vs. missed-context rate (the static-policy trade-off from arXiv:2604.14222).

4. **Test breadth (high-k single shot) vs. depth (iterative refinement) head-to-head on a synthesis prompt** (e.g., "list all Volere sections that bear on a quality requirement and their fit-criterion guidance"). The knowledge-model cross-reference graph (#6562) gives a ground-truth answer set to score recall against — a rare clean eval for breadth.

5. **Confidence-triggered retrieval as a cheap baseline.** Before each query, have the model self-assess whether it already knows the Volere field; only query on low confidence (FLARE/Self-RAG style). Compare against always-query to quantify the noise/cost saving on shallow sub-tasks.

6. **Generalize cautiously.** Requirements work is the *low-ambiguity, high-citation* corner of the task space; do not let its "fewer, sharper queries" optimum leak into the maths/coding lanes, which the cited literature shows want iterative multi-hop retrieval. Keep the router per-task-family, not global.
