# AutoSurvey and Academic Survey-Generation Systems: Retrieval Strategy, Citation Density, and Evaluation

**Sources:** Wang et al., "AutoSurvey: Large Language Models Can Automatically Write Surveys," arXiv:2406.10252 (NeurIPS 2024); Chen et al., "SurveyGen-I," arXiv:2508.14317 (IJCNLP 2025); "SurGE: A Benchmark and Evaluation Framework for Scientific Survey Generation," arXiv:2508.15658 (Tsinghua, 2025); "IterSurvey / Deep Literature Survey Automation with an Iterative Workflow," arXiv:2510.21900; "SciSage," arXiv:2506.12689; "SurveyG," arXiv:2510.07733; "SurveyEval," arXiv:2512.02763. Figures are quoted from these.

## 1. Two retrieval regimes, and why per-section wins on long outputs

AutoSurvey is the canonical pipeline and it uses **both** retrieval regimes at different stages. Upfront: it retrieves **1,200 topic-relevant papers (abstracts only)**, chunked into a 30k-token window, to build the *outline*. Then per-subsection: during drafting it retrieves **60 papers per subsection**, conditioned on the subsection description, using each paper's body (≤1,500 tokens). The outline retrieval is broad-and-shallow (breadth); the drafting retrieval is narrow-and-deep (depth), and it is re-issued once per subsection rather than once per survey.

The payoff is robustness to length. **Naive RAG** (one upfront retrieval, then generate) shows citation recall *decaying* with output length: 79.67% @ 8k → 71.48% @ 16k → **68.79% @ 64k** tokens. AutoSurvey's per-subsection retrieval holds **flat**: 82.48% / 81.34% / 83.14% / **82.25%** across 8k–64k (precision ~77% throughout; human baseline 86.33% recall / 77.78% precision). The lesson is mechanistic, not cosmetic: a single upfront context cannot stay relevant across a long, multi-topic generation — relevance must be *refreshed per local writing target*. Later systems push this further. **IterSurvey** (2510.21900) argues the one-shot paradigm causes "noisy retrieval, fragmented structures, and context overload," and replaces it with a planning agent that *recurrently* retrieves-reads-updates the outline; using "paper cards" (structured contribution/method/finding abstractions) it lifts recall to **0.70 at equal precision** vs AutoSurvey. **SurveyGen-I** (2508.14317) uses coarse-to-fine retrieval that fires fine-grained subsection-level retrieval *only when it detects insufficient context* — a conditional, demand-driven refinement rather than unconditional per-section re-query.

## 2. Citation density is a first-class, separable metric

SurveyGen-I formalizes three reference-level metrics: **Number of References (NR)** = distinct works cited (coverage breadth); **Citation Density (CD)** = unique citation markers per character of body text (integration intensity); and a recency metric. Against the strongest baseline it reports **+27% citation density, >2× distinct references, and better recency** — i.e., density and count move somewhat independently of content-quality scores (+8.5%). This matters: citation *quality* (recall/precision against a ground-truth reference set, per SurveyEval/AutoSurvey) and citation *density* (how thickly claims are grounded in-text) are different axes. A system can be precise but sparse. The ablation isolates the cause cleanly: **removing retrieval drops AutoSurvey recall 83.48%→60.11% and precision 77.15%→51.65%**, whereas **removing the reflection/refinement pass barely moves citation numbers** (recall 83.48%→83.23%). Grounding density is bought by retrieval, not by post-hoc polishing.

## 3. Evaluation: LLM-as-judge correlates with humans only moderately, and self-defined metrics are biased

AutoSurvey's own meta-evaluation reports a **mixture-of-three-judges Spearman ρ = 0.5429** against human expert rankings — described as the *highest* among configurations, yet only a *moderate* correlation. SurGE (2508.15658) is the field's pushback: it argues AutoSurvey/SurveyForge use "custom metrics tailored to validate their own pipelines," a **self-validation paradigm that introduces bias and prevents fair cross-system comparison**, and instead supplies a 1M-paper corpus, held-out expert surveys, and a meta-evaluation where PhD reviewers blind-rank four anonymized surveys (3 systems + human ground truth) on structure and content. DeepSurvey-Bench similarly warns of LLM-judge **preference bias toward AI-generated surveys** and triangulates with percent-agreement, Spearman, and Cohen's κ. Net: judge scores are usable as a *relative* signal but must be (a) computed by a model family distinct from the generator, (b) validated against held-out human rankings, and (c) reported with citation recall/precision against a ground-truth set, never only self-defined scores.

## 4. Actionable implications for task-conditioned librarian experiments

These systems are the closest published analogue to "literature synthesis as a *task type*," and they directly inform how the librarian should be driven for synthesis vs. point lookup.

1. **Test per-section vs. upfront retrieval as the headline synthesis variable.** For a multi-section synthesis answer, compare (a) one upfront k=20 query, then write everything, against (b) one query *per planned subsection*. AutoSurvey predicts (b) holds grounding flat as the answer lengthens while (a) decays — measure citation recall/density per 1k tokens of output to catch the decay curve.

2. **Make "outline first, then retrieve per node" the synthesis protocol.** AutoSurvey/IterSurvey both retrieve broad+shallow (abstracts/breadcrumbs) to plan, then narrow+deep (full chunks) per node. Our breadcrumb metadata is the natural breadth signal; full chunks are the depth signal. Experiment: does a cheap breadcrumb-only scout pass improve final coverage vs. jumping straight to k=20 chunk retrieval.

3. **Report citation density and distinct-source count, not just recall.** A synthesis can be hallucination-free (our abstention contract) yet under-grounded. Add CD (citation markers per 100 words) and distinct `source_id` count as synthesis-task metrics; sparse-but-correct should score worse than dense-but-correct.

4. **Use a conditional refinement trigger, not unconditional re-query.** SurveyGen-I retrieves more *only on detected insufficient context*; PaperQA2 (sibling note) averages ~1.26 searches. The stopping rule for synthesis should be evidence-count + source-diversity based (e.g., "≥N distinct sources covering each subclaim, else re-query that subclaim"), not a fixed search budget.

5. **Separate the judge from the generator and validate against humans.** When we score librarian-assisted synthesis quality with an LLM judge, use a different model family, anchor to a small held-out human-ranked set, and treat ρ≈0.5 as the realistic ceiling — do not over-trust a single judge score.

6. **Length is the stress test, not difficulty.** The AutoSurvey/naive-RAG gap only opens up at long outputs (16k+ tokens). Task-conditioned experiments should include a long-synthesis condition; short Q&A will not reveal whether per-section retrieval is worth its cost.
