# Task-Conditioned Retrieval Granularity: When Sections Beat Chunks

**Scope.** Round 1 fixed the librarian's *single-query mechanics* (verbatim query, k=20, quote-first, abstention). This note asks the orthogonal question: for a *given kind of work*, what should be returned (atomic chunk vs. whole section/chapter) and *how should the assistant drive the tool* (one shot vs. iterate, breadth vs. depth, when to stop). The published evidence is strong that the answer is task-dependent, not a single global setting.

## Granularity is not a fixed parameter — it is task-conditioned

The foundational result is Chen et al.'s *Dense X Retrieval* (EMNLP 2024): the **retrieval unit** (document / passage / sentence / proposition) "significantly impacts the performance of both retrieval and downstream tasks." On fact-dense Wikipedia QA, indexing by **propositions** (atomic, self-contained factoids) beat 100-word passages and sentences on Recall@5 and Exact Match across five open-domain QA sets, *under a fixed token budget* — finer units pack more relevance per token ([arXiv 2312.06648](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.06648)). The same paper flags the boundary condition: propositions excel for "fact-based texts" but lose "flow and contextual continuity" on narrative text — exactly the regime where a derivation or an argument must be read whole.

The opposite extreme is equally evidenced. **LongRAG** (TIGER-Lab) uses *long* retrieval units — whole documents (>4K tokens) or hyperlink-grouped document groups (~6K-token average) — compressing the corpus ~30x (22M→600K units) and lifting top-1 answer recall ~20 points (52.2→71.7); it needs ~10x fewer units and reaches EM 62.7 (NQ) / 64.3 (HotpotQA) with no training ([arXiv 2406.15319](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.15319)). So **coarse units win when the answer is spread across or needs the surrounding argument; fine units win when the answer is a localizable fact.**

The reconciliation in production is **decoupling the retrieval unit from the generation unit**: embed small (precise matching), return large (context). This is LangChain's `ParentDocumentRetriever` and LlamaIndex's small-to-big / sentence-window retrievers — search on 400-token children, hand the LLM the 2000-token parent (or the whole doc) ([LangChain docs](https://python.langchain.com/docs/how_to/parent_document_retriever/), [LlamaIndex](https://docs.llamaindex.ai/en/stable/examples/retrievers/multi_doc_together_hybrid/)). This maps cleanly onto the librarian's existing breadcrumb metadata: a chunk hit can be expanded to its section/chapter when the task needs flow.

## Coding and budget-scaling: granularity should track the context budget

A controlled code-RAG study (Function/Declaration/Sliding-Window/cAST × 4 retrievers × 5 generators, 864 settings) plus a task-aware compute-budget study give the cleanest dose-response: **optimal chunk size scales with the available context window** — 32-64 lines best at ≤4000 tokens, 64-128 lines as budget grows, **whole-file retrieval only competitive at ~16000 tokens** ([arXiv 2510.20609](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.20609), [2605.04763](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.04763v1)). Modality also flips the retriever: BM25/lexical wins code→code; dense/semantic wins NL→code (bug localization), e.g. Voyage-3-code ≈0.72 NDCG vs BM25 ≈0.57. Implication for an *API/signature* lookup: small structure-aware unit (keep the function/class intact); for *understanding a subsystem*: parent/file expansion.

## More context is not free — stopping rules matter

Liu et al.'s *Lost in the Middle* (TACL 2024) is the constraint that bounds "just return more": a U-shaped curve where relevant info in the middle is under-used, and reader accuracy **saturates well before retriever recall** — beyond ~20 documents, GPT-3.5 gains ~1.5% / Claude ~1% while latency and cost climb ([arXiv 2307.03172](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172)). This independently re-derives Round 1's k≈20 ceiling and argues *against* expanding every hit to a full chapter by default — coarse expansion must be selective or it floods the middle.

For *multi-step* work (synthesis, multi-hop derivations) the literature favors **iterate, don't widen**: IRCoT / Iter-RetGen interleave reasoning and retrieval; Plan-RAG decomposes into sub-questions. The open problem is *when to stop*: **Stop-RAG** (Oct 2025) shows fixed-iteration and confidence-proxy stopping both underperform a learned margin rule (stop if Q(STOP)−Q(CONT) > threshold) on MuSiQue/HotpotQA/2WikiMultihop ([arXiv 2510.14337](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.14337)). The cheap proxy the librarian already emits — the **confidence label** — is the natural stand-in for that value signal.

## Actionable implications for librarian usage experiments

1. **Test a two-tier return, not one granularity.** Default to chunk hits (Round-1 k=20); add an *expand-to-section/chapter* action driven by breadcrumbs. Hypothesis: expansion helps derivations/narrative (particle-physics method sections, multi-step proofs), hurts or is neutral for API/fact lookups (Dense X vs. LongRAG split).
2. **Condition the strategy on a task label.** Pre-classify the query into {fact/API, derivation/proof, literature synthesis, learning/explanation, writing}. Measure whether *fixed* per-class strategies beat one global strategy — this is the core experiment.
3. **Breadth-then-depth vs. depth-first as an arm.** Fact tasks: single verbatim query, no expansion (cheapest point). Synthesis/multi-hop: iterative sub-queries with section expansion on the top 1-2 hits only (guard the middle).
4. **Make the confidence label the stopping signal.** Operationalize a Stop-RAG-style rule: stop iterating / stop expanding once a "high-confidence" hit covers the claim; keep going on "low". Compare against fixed-iteration baselines.
5. **Scale expansion to the budget, not the question.** Borrow the code-RAG dose-response: small budget → chunks only; large budget → allow whole-section units. Sweep expansion-on/off across context budgets.
6. **Guard against lost-in-the-middle when expanding.** If expanding hits raises returned tokens past the ~20-chunk equivalent, rerank/truncate rather than concatenate; track answer accuracy vs. position of the load-bearing passage.
