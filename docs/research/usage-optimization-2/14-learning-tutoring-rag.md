# Pedagogy-Aware RAG: How a Tutor Should Pull From Textbooks Differently Than a QA Bot

**Sources (quoted/cited below):** Frontiers in Computer Science, "Intelligent tutoring system based on multimodal knowledge graphs and RAG," 10.3389/fcomp.2026.1777749 (2026); Levonian et al., "Retrieval-augmented Generation to Improve Math Question-Answering," arXiv:2310.03184 (2023, ERIC ED675655); Jeong et al., "Adaptive-RAG," NAACL 2024 (arXiv:2403.14403); "KITE" retrieval-augmented tutor (arXiv:2605.12988); TreeInstruct / "Instruct, Not Assist" Socratic debugging (arXiv:2406.11709); Sweller & Cooper worked-example effect; Kalyuga et al. (2003) expertise-reversal effect; Renkl et al. (2002) guidance fading.

## 1. The central result: factual correctness ≠ pedagogical optimality

Every serious tutoring-RAG paper converges on the same finding: grounding an answer in retrieved textbook text makes it *correct* but not necessarily *instructive*, and over-grounding can actively hurt. The Frontiers ITS paper names this "the gap between factual correctness and pedagogical optimality. Retrieved content, though accurate, may not constitute the most instructive explanation for a learner's specific context," and decomposes it into two failure modes a QA bot does not have: **logical brevity vs. scaffolding** ("graph-retrieved facts... may present a condensed 'expert' view lacking the step-by-step elaboration a novice needs") and **semantic relevance vs. curricular alignment** (topically-related chunks "might introduce concepts beyond the current learning objective... causing distraction or cognitive overload").

The sharpest empirical confirmation is Levonian et al. on middle-school math QA: human raters "prefer responses generated using RAG, **but not when responses are too grounded in the textbook content.**" This is the inverse of the Round-1 quote-first finding — for a QA bot, maximal grounding maximizes precision; for a tutor, maximal grounding *reduces* preference because verbatim source text is pitched at the wrong level. So the quote-first contract that won Round-1 is task-conditioned: it is right for synthesis/factual lookup and wrong (or needs a transformation layer) for tutoring generation.

## 2. The pedagogical principle that should drive *retrieval mode*: expertise reversal

Cognitive Load Theory supplies the rule for *what* to retrieve. The **worked-example effect** (Sweller & Cooper): novices "who studied worked examples performed significantly better than learners who actively solved problems," because unguided solving overloads working memory and blocks schema formation. But the **expertise-reversal effect** (Kalyuga, Ayres, Chandler & Sweller 2003) is the boundary: "the heavy use of worked examples becomes less and less effective as learners' expertise increases, eventually becoming redundant or even counter-productive." Hence **guidance fading** (Renkl et al. 2002): scaffold every step, then fade — backward fading (drop the last step first) beat forward fading and produced reliable **near-transfer** gains. The operational warning for us: Sweller notes the effects "rely heavily on instructors picking the right point at which to alter instructional techniques. If the wrong point is picked, the effects are unlikely to be obtained," and fading too early underperforms sustained scaffolding. A tutor therefore needs a learner-expertise signal to choose *worked-example retrieval (novice)* vs. *prerequisite/definition retrieval + problem (expert)* — a parameter a QA bot ignores entirely.

## 3. Routing and intent: the architecture both literatures point to

Tutoring-RAG and general adaptive-RAG independently arrive at **query-intent routing**. Jeong et al.'s **Adaptive-RAG** trains a T5-Large classifier to route each query to **no-retrieval / single-step / multi-step** by predicted complexity (labels auto-derived: no-retrieval-correct = low; only multi-step-correct = high), matching always-expensive baselines at much lower cost. KITE applies the same idea pedagogically — "grounded explanations for factual questions and Socratic scaffolding for procedural and reasoning tasks" — and experts rated **93.18%** of its feedback positively for scaffolding. The Frontiers hybrid fuses **graph (prerequisite/relational, via Cypher) + vector (semantic)** retrieval with weighted scoring `S = α·S_g + (1-α)·S_v + β·I`, beating text-only RAG on accuracy (**1.82 vs 1.58 /2.0**), explainability (**1.76 vs 1.32**), and especially multi-step reasoning (**1.86 vs 1.16**). Socratic systems add the opposite constraint: existing assistive tutors "directly reveal the code answer 57% of the time" (CodeAid), so a tutor must sometimes retrieve *to withhold* — pulling a hint or the next prerequisite, not the solution (TreeInstruct's dynamic question tree).

## 4. Actionable implications for task-conditioned librarian experiments

1. **Add a "learning" task mode that inverts the quote-first contract.** For tutoring queries, retrieve broad but generate a *transformed* answer (elaborated worked example or Socratic hint), not a verbatim quote. Experiment: A/B quote-first vs. quote-then-elaborate on learner-rated helpfulness, expecting the Levonian inversion (over-grounding hurts).

2. **Condition retrieval target on an expertise signal.** Add a lightweight learner-level input (novice/intermediate/expert). Route novice → worked-example/derivation chunks; expert → definition + prerequisite-edge chunks + open problem. Measure transfer, and test the fading hypothesis (sustained scaffolding beats early fading for novices).

3. **Build a 3-way intent router à la Adaptive-RAG, but keyed to task not just complexity.** Classes: factual-lookup (k≈5, quote-first, single search), procedural/learning (retrieve worked examples + prerequisites, possibly multi-search to assemble a fade sequence), Socratic (retrieve-to-withhold). Benchmark routed vs. fixed single-shot.

4. **Exploit breadcrumbs as a prerequisite graph.** The Frontiers graph+vector fusion (+0.24 accuracy, +0.44 explainability over text-only) suggests our breadcrumb metadata can act as a poor-man's knowledge graph: when a chunk presupposes a concept, follow the breadcrumb *upward* to retrieve the prerequisite. Test this "prerequisite traversal" against flat top-k for learning queries.

5. **Add a "don't reveal the answer" stopping/filtering rule for Socratic mode** and measure answer-leakage rate against the 57% CodeAid baseline — a tutoring-specific failure mode the abstention contract does not cover.

6. **Track learner-rated helpfulness and cognitive-alignment separately from precision/recall.** The whole literature shows pedagogical value is orthogonal to factual precision; our eval must add a level-appropriateness/scaffolding rating, not just the unsupported-claim rate Round-1 optimized.
