# Fact-Checking Pipelines: Decompose -> Retrieve -> Verdict, and What It Teaches Task-Conditioned Librarian Use

## Scope

Automated fact-checking (AFC) is the closest published analogue to what an AI assistant does when it grounds a claim against the librarian: take an assertion, find supporting passages, and decide whether the passages support it. The canonical AFC pipeline is three stages — **claim decomposition -> evidence retrieval (document then sentence/passage selection) -> verdict (NLI/entailment)** — and the literature has measured, at each stage, *how much* evidence to gather and *how often* that gathering helps. Those measured numbers transfer directly to designing per-task usage strategies for our RAG tool.

## Key findings with numbers

**The pipeline is standard and retrieval is the bottleneck.** FEVER (185k claims over a 2017 Wikipedia dump) established the document-retrieval -> sentence-selection -> textual-entailment decomposition and the dual metric: *Label Accuracy* (verdict only) vs the stricter *FEVER score* (verdict AND correct evidence) ([Thorne et al. 2018, FEVER](https://aclanthology.org/N18-1074/)). The original gap is the lesson: 50.9% label-only accuracy collapses to 31.9% when correct evidence is required, and an oracle with gold documents + perfect entailment reaches only ~70%. On complex real-world claims, retrieval, not reasoning, caps performance — 36% of questions were unanswerable from retrieved web evidence ([Chen et al. 2023, complex claim verification](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.11859)). Implication: for the librarian, retrieval quality and the abstention contract dominate end-to-end correctness, exactly as Round 1 found (12%->0% hallucination).

**How many evidence units per claim.** The AVeriTeC web baseline picks **N=3** sentences per claim after a coarse BM25 top-100 filter, and uses **10** in-context examples to generate search questions (tested {1,3,5,10}, 10 best) ([Schlichtkrull et al. 2023, AVeriTeC](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.13117)); a simple RAG re-framing of this pipeline scored 50.4% AVeriTeC end-to-end ([Ullrich et al. 2024, AIC CTU](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.11446)). FEVER systems likewise select top-k sentences with k small (typically 3-5). For long-form factuality, **SAFE** decomposes a response into atomic facts and issues a *default 5 search queries per fact, 3 results each*; an ablation showed correlation with humans peaks at 5 queries and **does not improve past that point** — extra queries only add cost ([Wei et al. 2024, SAFE / long-form factuality](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2403.18802)). SAFE agrees with crowd annotators 72% of the time (76% on adjudicated disagreements) at >20x lower cost; an independent benchmark put it at 84.5% vs FActScore's 70.0%, but at $0.49 and 28 min per response. Notably a competitor matched SAFE with **1 query per fact** against Wikipedia, because curated sources give holistic context where web snippets are fragmentary — depth of source beats breadth of search.

**Decomposition helps conditionally, not universally.** Atomic-fact decomposition (FActScore, FacTool, SAFE) enables targeted retrieval and error localization, and FActScore showed even single sentences mix supported/unsupported facts (40% of ChatGPT sentences) ([Min et al. 2023, FActScore](https://aclanthology.org/2023.emnlp-main.741/)). But MiniCheck found **near-zero gain for GPT-4 and mixed gains** for specialized checkers from decomposition, with no consistent improvement ([Tang et al. 2024, MiniCheck](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2404.10774)). Over-decomposition strips context and causes attribution errors; "molecular facts" argue for the *minimal self-contained* unit, not the smallest ([Gunjal & Durrett 2024, Molecular Facts](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2406.20079)). Effectiveness is "heavily dependent on the decomposition strategy," not the act of decomposing.

**Depth (hops) should be matched to claim complexity.** HoVer (claims spanning up to 4 Wikipedia articles) showed verdict accuracy degrades systematically as hops and supporting-document count rise ([Jiang et al. 2020, HoVer](https://aclanthology.org/2020.findings-emnlp.309/)). Recent hop-count ablations: optimal retrieval depth is **2 hops on FEVER** (most claims need 1-2 sentences) but **2/3/4 hops on HoVer's 2/3/4-hop subsets** respectively ([Reasoning Paths as Signals 2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.07075v1)). Iterative retrieval (MDR, Baleen) suffers error propagation — early-hop mistakes are irreversible — so more depth is only worth it when the claim genuinely needs composition.

**Stopping rules: sufficiency is a set-level property, not per-passage relevance.** "When to retrieve" is solved by confidence triggers (FLARE: retrieve on low token probability; DRAGIN) and learned reflection tokens (Self-RAG) ([RAG survey](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2312.10997)). "When is evidence *enough*" is harder: relevance is not justification, and per-step confidence is an unreliable completeness proxy. Dedicated sufficiency judges (S2G-RAG reports a 6.44% false-positive sufficiency rate; SURE-RAG verifies support/refute/insufficient and abstains) and formal entropy-threshold stopping (ECR: stop when H(A|X) <= epsilon) outperform self-assessment. The two failure modes are *over-confident finalize* and *under-confident continue* — the latter wastes searches and injects distractor noise.

## Actionable implications for librarian usage experiments

1. **Budget search count by task, and cap it.** SAFE's flat-after-5 curve and AVeriTeC's k=3 say diminishing returns arrive fast. Experiment: literature-synthesis tasks get a higher query budget (multi-source composition, HoVer-like), while a single maths/definition lookup gets 1-2 — measure where each task's accuracy curve flattens, mirroring Round 1's k=20/k=8 finding at the *number-of-searches* level rather than the k level.

2. **Make decomposition a task-conditioned switch, not a default.** Decompose only for multi-part literature/science claims; for a single coding/API or maths fact, query verbatim (Round 1) without atomization. Test decompose-vs-not per task type; expect MiniCheck-style null results on simple tasks and HoVer-style gains on compositional ones.

3. **Match depth to claim structure.** Add an iterative/follow-up-query arm only for multi-hop synthesis questions; measure error propagation (does a wrong hop-1 chunk poison the verdict?) against single-shot k=20.

4. **Add an explicit set-level sufficiency/stopping rule.** Reuse the abstention contract as a *stopping* signal, not just a hallucination guard: after each retrieval, judge support / refute / insufficient over the *accumulated* chunk set (SURE-RAG style), and stop on "sufficient." Track false-positive sufficiency (premature stop) and wasted-continue rates as headline metrics.

5. **Prefer source depth over search breadth.** The 1-query-Wikipedia result beating 5-query-web argues the librarian's curated, breadcrumbed chunks may need *fewer* searches than open web — quantify this advantage explicitly.

6. **Score evidence-conditioned, not label-only.** Adopt a FEVER-score analogue (correct answer AND cited the right chunk) so experiments reward grounded verdicts, exposing the retrieval-bound ceiling per task.
