# Query Reformulation Trajectories of Expert Searchers: How Queries Evolve Across a Session

**Sources:** Jansen, Booth & Spink, "Patterns and transitions of query reformulation during web searching" (*IP&M* 45(2), 2009; Dogpile.com, 2.46M interactions / 534K users); Huang & Efthimiadis, "Analyzing and evaluating query reformulation strategies in web search logs" (*CIKM* 2009; AOL logs; taxonomy + rule-based classifier); Boldi et al., "Query reformulation mining: models, patterns, and applications" (*Inf. Retr.* 14, 2011; ~17M reformulations, 4-class model @92%); Liu et al., "Analysis of query reformulation types on different search tasks" (2010; 48 users × 6 tasks, Simple/Hierarchical/Parallel); "Towards a Better Understanding of Query Reformulation Behavior in Web Search" (*WWW* 2021; Specialization→Intent-Shift two-phase model + domain-expertise analysis); Hölscher & Strube, "Web search behavior of internet experts and newbies" (*WWW9*, 2000); Aula, Khan & Guan, "How does search behavior change as search becomes more difficult?" (*CHI* 2010); Hearst, *Search User Interfaces* Ch.6 (CUP, 2009; AltaVista/Prisma/Dogpile figures); Bates "berrypicking" (1989); RQ-RAG (Chan et al., arXiv:2404.00610); IterDRAG / "Inference Scaling for Long-Context RAG" (Yue et al., arXiv:2410.04343); Adaptive-RAG (Jeong et al., NAACL 2024); EfficientRAG (arXiv:2408.04259).

## 1. Reformulation is the norm, and term retention — not wholesale rewriting — is the mechanism

Across large logs, **~50% of sessions reformulate** at least once: a 3M-record AltaVista study found 52% of users modified the query and 32% issued ≥3 queries (Hearst Ch.6). The *micro-move* is overwhelmingly conservative editing, not a fresh start. Anick's Prisma analysis decomposes reformulations into adding/changing modifiers (25%), elaborating with new facets (24%), adding head terms (15%), and rephrasing the same concept (12%). This is the empirical basis for Round-1's verbatim-query win: experts mutate the *content words* while preserving the topical core, so the embedding of query N+1 sits near query N — the opposite of HyDE/rewrite, which discards the anchor. The librarian analogue is **"edit, don't restart": carry the load-bearing nouns across searches and swap one facet at a time.**

## 2. The two canonical trajectory moves: specialization (narrow) and generalization (broaden)

Jansen et al.'s Dogpile study is the canonical trajectory map. Within reformulating sessions: same-topic reformulation 22.7%, system-assistance 22.2%, **specialization 16.3%** (+9.9% specialization-with-reformulation), **generalization 7.2%** (+9.8% generalization-with-reformulation). Two facts matter for us: (a) **specialization is ~2× more common than generalization** as a deliberate move — searchers default to narrowing; (b) the strongest *transition* pattern they identify is oscillation **between specialization and generalization** — searchers ping-pong, not monotonically narrow. Boldi et al. confirm this is learnable and large-scale: a 4-class model (generalization / specialization / error-correction / **parallel move** — a same-level lateral shift to a sibling concept) hits **92% accuracy** over ~17M reformulations, and the *transition graph between these classes* (not the marginal frequencies) is what predicts intent. The actionable shape: the librarian's refinement repertoire is not "rewrite" but a small typed set — **narrow, broaden, lateral-sibling, fix-error** — each with a different trigger.

## 3. The session arc: Specialization → Intent-Shift (broad first, narrow later, then jump)

The WWW'21 study characterises the *whole-session* trajectory as a two-phase process: **Specialization → Intent-Shift**. Early queries progressively narrow within one intent; once that intent is exhausted, the searcher makes a discrete *jump* to a new sub-intent (a parallel move at session scale). Their design corollary is directly portable: **query recommendations should be broad early and narrowed as follow-ups arrive** — i.e., scope is a function of session position. Aula, Khan & Guan (CHI'10) give the difficulty-modulated version: as a task gets harder, searchers issue *more* queries, use *more diverse* query vocabulary, and spend more time per query — difficulty inflates the trajectory rather than changing its direction. Together these say the librarian should treat *session position and observed difficulty* as inputs: open wide, tighten on success, and recognise an exhausted intent as the cue to jump rather than keep narrowing a dry vein.

## 4. Expertise changes the trajectory, but less than folklore claims — domain knowledge > web-search skill

Hölscher & Strube (WWW9, 2000) characterise experts by **fluid switching** between searching and browsing and more deliberate query planning. But the *number of queries per session* discriminator is genuinely contested across studies (inconsistent expert definitions, small n). The sharper, more reliable signal is **domain** expertise: WWW'21 found search-engine expertise has weak effects, whereas **in-domain users formulate more specialized queries up-front, drawing less on engine suggestions** and narrowing the scope faster from their own knowledge. This is the single most important transfer to the librarian: **Claude is effectively the "in-domain expert"** — it can and should issue precise, terminology-loaded queries immediately rather than starting broad-and-vague and leaning on relevance feedback, *for tasks where it holds the vocabulary* (software-engineering terms, detector physics). The corollary risk is over-specialization on tasks where it does *not* hold the vocabulary (an unfamiliar sub-field), where a deliberate broaden-first arc is safer.

## 5. Task type dictates which move dominates

Liu et al. (48 users × 6 tasks) tie reformulation type to task structure: **specialization dominates Simple tasks; word-substitution (a parallel/lateral move) dominates Parallel tasks**; hierarchical tasks mix both. Berrypicking (Bates 1989) is the extreme exploratory case — the query *evolves continuously* and evidence is gathered bit-at-a-time across a meandering path, so lateral moves and re-anchoring dominate over clean narrowing. The modern agentic literature has rediscovered exactly this typing: Adaptive-RAG routes by query complexity (no-retrieval / single-step / multi-step); RQ-RAG learns three explicit reformulation operators — **rewrite, decompose, disambiguate**; IterDRAG caps decomposition at **≤5 iterations**; EfficientRAG shows iterative decompose-then-retrieve hits the recall of one-shot 200-chunk retrieval at **~20 chunks**, i.e., a *trajectory* of small targeted queries beats one fat query. This maps task→trajectory: lookup/maths/API = single specialized query (no trajectory); synthesis/4D-STEM-style investigation = decompose into sub-queries + lateral berrypicking; learning = broaden-first then specialize.

## 6. Actionable implications for task-conditioned librarian experiments

1. **Benchmark a typed reformulation repertoire, not free rewriting.** Constrain the agent's follow-up search to {specialize, generalize, lateral-sibling, fix-term} and measure answer quality vs. unconstrained rewrite. Hypothesis (from §1–2): typed term-preserving edits beat free rewrites, extending Round-1's verbatim win into the multi-search regime.
2. **Test the broad→narrow→jump arc explicitly per task.** For synthesis tasks, seed search 1 deliberately broad, specialize on hits, and trigger a *parallel/intent-shift* search when marginal new evidence stalls (ties to §20's marginal-gain stop). Compare against a flat "N independent narrow queries" baseline; the WWW'21 prediction is the arc wins on coverage.
3. **Condition opening-query specificity on Claude's domain vocabulary, not on a global default.** When the task vocabulary is held (SE/detector terms), issue a precise specialized query first (the in-domain-expert move, §4); when it is not, broaden first. Operationalise "vocabulary held" via the confidence label on a probe query and measure searches-to-answer for each branch.
4. **Map task family → expected trajectory and validate against the ~1.26-search PaperQA2 baseline.** Lookup/maths/coding → 1 specialized query, no trajectory; synthesis/investigation → decompose + berrypick (cap iterations à la IterDRAG ≤5); learning → broaden-then-specialize. Log the realized trajectory type and check it matches the predicted family.
5. **Exploit breadcrumbs as the berrypicking / lateral-move primitive.** §5's parallel moves and Bates' bit-at-a-time gathering correspond to traversing a chunk's breadcrumb neighbourhood instead of re-querying. Experiment: breadcrumb-hop vs. new lateral query for sibling-concept coverage; measure distinct-source coverage and latency.
6. **Detect "exhausted intent" as a first-class signal, distinct from the stop decision.** Specialization that stops moving the answer is the cue to *jump* (new sub-intent), not necessarily to *stop* (§20). Instrument representational-stability over reformulations: stable answer + remaining task scope ⇒ intent-shift search; stable answer + scope satisfied ⇒ stop.
