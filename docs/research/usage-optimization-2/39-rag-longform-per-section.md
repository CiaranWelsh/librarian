# Retrieval Timing for Long-Form Generation: Per-Section/Per-Claim vs Single Upfront Retrieval, and How It Should Be Task-Conditioned

**Sources:** FLARE / Active Retrieval (Jiang et al., EMNLP 2023, [arXiv:2305.06983](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.06983)); Adaptive-RAG (Jeong et al., NAACL 2024, [arXiv:2403.14403](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2403.14403)); ALCE citation benchmark (Gao et al., EMNLP 2023, [arXiv:2305.14627](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2305.14627)); OpenScholar (Asai et al., Nature 2025 / [arXiv:2411.14199](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.14199v1)); Self-RAG (Asai et al., ICLR 2024); ITER-RETGEN / ITRG (iterative retrieve-generate); Mallen et al. 2023 (popularity-gated retrieval); QHackBench code-RAG ([arXiv:2506.20008](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.20008)); RAG-noise-robustness survey ([arXiv:2506.00054](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.00054v1)).

## 1. The core dichotomy and when each wins

Single-upfront ("retrieve-then-generate") issues one query on the input, concatenates top-k, and writes the whole answer. Per-section/per-claim ("active" or "iterative") interleaves retrieval with generation, re-querying as the information need evolves. The settled empirical pattern: **iterative retrieval helps in proportion to how much the information need shifts during writing, and how many disjoint facts the answer must stitch together.** FLARE — the canonical per-sentence method, which regenerates a sentence only when a token's probability drops below threshold θ — beats single-time retrieval **decisively where the need is multi-hop and nearly not at all where it isn't**: on 2WikiMultihopQA FLARE-direct hits **51.0 EM vs 39.4** for single retrieval (vs 28.2 no-retrieval), but on ASQA the gap collapses to **41.3 vs 40.0** and on WikiAsp it is a near-tie (UniEval 53.4 vs 52.4) ([arXiv:2305.06983](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.06983)). The lesson is not "iterate by default" but "iterate when the upfront query cannot anticipate the evidence the later prose will need."

## 2. The decision is fundamentally task-/complexity-conditioned

Adaptive-RAG makes the routing explicit and is the most directly transferable result. A lightweight T5-Large classifier sorts each query into **no-retrieval / single-step / multi-step**, trained on automatically-derived labels (from whether models actually answered, plus the dataset's single-vs-multi-hop prior). It matches the always-multi-step pipeline's accuracy at roughly **half the retrieval steps** — e.g. 56.8 Acc at **1.59 steps** vs the multi-step 62.4 Acc at **3.36 steps** on one dataset — and dominates other adaptive baselines (47.0 vs Self-RAG's 30.6 Acc) ([arXiv:2403.14403](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2403.14403)). Two findings matter for us: (a) the **Oracle** router (perfect classification) outperforms everything on both axes, so *classifier accuracy is the whole bottleneck* — the routing decision is where the value is; and (b) classifier size barely moved results, so the task-conditioning signal is cheap to extract. Mallen et al. add the complementary axis: gate retrieval on **entity popularity** — models reliably know head facts and need retrieval mainly for long-tail ones, so retrieving on well-known content can be net-harmful. Across this literature the "when/how-much to retrieve" signal is consistently one of: question complexity (Adaptive-RAG), popularity/parametric confidence (Mallen, Self-RAG), token uncertainty (FLARE), or evidence sufficiency (CRAG/SIM-RAG).

## 3. Citation quality is a separate axis from coverage

Iterating improves *coverage* of facts; it does not automatically improve *attribution*. ALCE formalised citation **recall** (do the cited chunks entail the sentence?) and **precision** (does removing any one cited chunk break entailment?), measured by an NLI model, and showed top systems still leave large attribution gaps even when answer correctness is fine ([arXiv:2305.14627](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/2305.14627)). Notably ALCE feeds only a **small in-context set (~4-5 passages)** despite a top-100 retrieval pool — evidence that beyond a point more passages hurt attribution, not help it. OpenScholar is the strongest demonstration that the *iterate-with-self-feedback* loop fixes citations specifically: over 45M papers it drafts, emits natural-language feedback on missing/unsupported claims, retrieves more, and revises until satisfied. Result: it reaches **expert-level citation accuracy where GPT-4o hallucinates citations 78-90% of the time**, and bolting its retriever + feedback loop onto GPT-4o raised that model's **citation F1 from 0.1 to 39.5** and correctness +12% ([arXiv:2411.14199](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.14199v1)). So per-section retrieval's citation benefit comes specifically from the *feedback-triggered re-retrieval*, not from interleaving per se.

## 4. Over-retrieval and mis-aligned iteration actively harm reasoning/coding

The cost of getting the task-conditioning wrong is not just latency. For math and code, retrieval over a mismatched corpus frequently drops accuracy **below the no-retrieval baseline**: a QHackBench case shows retrieved floating-point caveats causing a model to over-engineer a tolerance loop and fail hidden tests, and class-level code studies find retrieval helps *only when the local spec is incomplete* — otherwise it is redundant or interferes with training priors ([arXiv:2506.20008](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.20008); robustness survey [arXiv:2506.00054](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.00054v1)). The general mechanisms — distraction, context-interference with priors, and noise amplification from adding documents — mean **the right move for reasoning-heavy tasks is fewer, higher-precision retrievals (or none), not more iterations.**

## 5. Actionable implications for task-conditioned librarian experiments

1. **Test a 3-way router as the central arm, mirroring Adaptive-RAG.** Per task type, pre-classify into *no-retrieval / single upfront / iterative-per-section*. Hypothesis from the literature: literature-synthesis and science-writing land on iterative; single-fact lookup and "known-item" questions land on single upfront; pure maths/coding land on no-retrieval-or-single-precise. Measure accuracy-vs-searches-per-task and confirm the router matches always-iterate quality at far lower search count (the half-the-steps result).
2. **Trigger re-retrieval on a confidence/sufficiency signal, not a fixed cadence.** The librarian already emits a confidence label per query (Round-1) and FLARE shows uncertainty is the right trigger. Re-query a new section only when the drafted span is low-confidence or the current chunks do not entail it — and measure that this beats both fixed single-shot and fixed per-paragraph retrieval on cost-adjusted quality.
3. **Score citation recall/precision (ALCE-style) separately from content correctness.** Iteration is predicted to lift coverage; citation precision may *fall* if later searches dump more chunks in context. Run an NLI/entailment check per cited chunk and watch for the over-retrieval cliff — expect a small in-context k (Round-1's k=8 value point, not k=20) to win on citation precision even where k=20 wins on recall.
4. **Make the OpenScholar feedback loop an explicit, capped arm for synthesis only.** Draft -> "what claims lack a supporting chunk?" -> targeted re-search -> revise, capped at 1-2 rounds. This is where the citation-F1 0.1->39.5 lift lives. Predict the loop is net-positive for software/physics *synthesis* and ~zero or negative for lookup/coding (matches the QHackBench harm and the reasoning-RAG findings).
5. **Per-section retrieval should re-query verbatim from the section's own draft text, not a global rewrite.** Reuse the Round-1 verbatim-beats-HyDE result: the natural query for section *i* is the topic sentence / low-confidence span of section *i*, issued fresh. Test verbatim-section-query vs a rewritten/expanded section query for citation recall.
6. **Add a "retrieval-necessity" gate for reasoning tasks to prevent below-baseline regressions.** For maths/coding prompts, default to no-retrieval unless the local spec is incomplete or an unfamiliar API/symbol appears (Mallen popularity logic; code-RAG "information gap"). Instrument how often retrieval *hurts* these tasks — a non-trivial harm rate would justify making the librarian opt-in rather than default for them.
