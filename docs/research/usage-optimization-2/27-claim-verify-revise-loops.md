# Draft -> Verify-Each-Claim -> Revise Loops: Post-hoc Verification Efficacy, Citation-Correction Rates, and Cost/Benefit

**Sources:** RARR (Gao et al., ACL 2023, [arXiv:2210.08726](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.08726)); Chain-of-Verification / CoVe (Dhuliawala et al., Findings ACL 2024, [arXiv:2309.11495](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.11495)); CiteFix (post-processing citation correction for RAG, [arXiv:2504.15629](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.15629)); Self-Refine (Madaan et al., NeurIPS 2023, [OpenReview S37hOerQLB](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=S37hOerQLB)); "LLMs Cannot Self-Correct Reasoning Yet" (Huang et al., ICLR 2024, [arXiv:2310.01798](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01798)); CRITIC (Gou et al., [arXiv:2305.11738](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.11738)); CorrectBench ([arXiv:2510.16062](https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/2510.16062)); SemanticCite; librarian corpus: *Agentic Design Patterns*, Ch.4 Reflection.

## 1. The pattern, and the one precondition that decides whether it works

The "draft -> verify each claim -> revise" loop is the post-hoc instance of the **Reflection pattern**: generate, critique against a signal, revise, optionally iterate. The single most robust finding across the literature is that the loop's payoff is governed almost entirely by *the quality of the verification signal*, not by the elaborateness of the revision. **Intrinsic** self-correction (model re-grades its own draft with no external evidence) is unreliable and often *harmful*: Huang et al. show GPT-3.5 corrected only 7.6% of wrong GSM8K answers while flipping 8.8% of correct ones to wrong — a net loss — and that GPT-4 mostly just retains its draft ([arXiv:2310.01798](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01798)). CRITIC reproduces this: self-verify/self-correct "can be remarkably unreliable" and degrades performance without external feedback ([arXiv:2305.11738](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.11738)). The corollary for the librarian is decisive: **a retrieval-grounded verification step is exactly the external signal that makes the loop work** — post-hoc verify-against-retrieved-evidence converts an unreliable self-edit into a RAG correction. This is the same logic our Round-1 abstention contract used to drive hallucination 12%->0%; the verify-revise loop generalises it from "don't assert unsupported" to "find the support, then fix the assertion."

## 2. Efficacy when the signal is retrieval (the numbers that transfer)

**RARR** (research-and-revise: per claim, generate verification questions, retrieve, run an *agreement gate*, and only edit on disagreement) raises attribution while preserving wording: attribution **35.4%->43.4%** on NQ, **24.5%->31.5%** on StrategyQA, **13.2%->28.3%** on QReCC, all while keeping intent-preservation >90% ([arXiv:2210.08726](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.08726)). The agreement gate matters — it edits only flagged claims, so cost scales with the *number of suspect claims*, not total length. **CoVe** drafts, plans verification questions, answers them *independently of the draft* (the anti-confirmation move), then revises: FActScore on long-form biographies **55.9 (baseline) -> 63.7 (factored) -> 71.4 (factor+revise)** (~28% relative), and on Wikidata list questions precision **0.17->0.36** with hallucinated entities falling **2.95->0.68 per answer** ([arXiv:2309.11495](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.11495)). The load-bearing detail: verification answered *independently* of the draft beats joint verification, because conditioning on the draft makes the verifier repeat the draft's errors. For the librarian this means **the verification query should be issued fresh (verbatim, per Round-1), not phrased as "is my draft right?"**

## 3. Citation correction specifically: cheap heuristics beat LLM re-judging

Citation correctness is a distinct axis from claim correctness — a claim can be true but cite the wrong chunk. **CiteFix** post-processes RAG output by re-matching each factual point to the best-supporting retrieved passage. Baseline generative-search citation accuracy is ~74%; in one model 80.6% of "unverifiable" facts were merely *mis-cited*, not hallucinated. CiteFix yields **+15.46% relative** overall accuracy, and the per-algorithm table is the actionable part ([arXiv:2504.15629](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.15629)):

| Correction method | MQLA gain | Latency / factual point |
|---|---|---|
| Keyword matching | +12.7% | 0.014 s |
| Keyword + semantic context | +15.5% | 0.015 s |
| Off-the-shelf BERTScore | +2.6% | 0.389 s |
| Fine-tuned BERTScore | +15.8% | 0.389 s |
| **LLM-based re-matching** | **+1.9%** | **1.586 s** |

The LLM-as-citation-judge is the *worst* performer and ~100x slower than keyword+semantic matching. Citation correction is largely a string/embedding alignment problem the librarian already has the materials for (chunk text + breadcrumbs), and CiteFix let a 12x-cheaper/3x-faster model match a larger one. **SemanticCite** extends this from "does the cited chunk exist / overlap" to four-way content verification (supported / partial / unsupported / uncertain) at 84% accuracy — the verdict schema the librarian's confidence label should emit per cited chunk.

## 4. Cost/benefit: gains are front-loaded; iteration depth is the cost knob

Self-Refine and CorrectBench both show **the first 1-2 revision rounds capture most of the gain, with sharp diminishing returns and rising "resource inefficiency" thereafter** ([OpenReview S37hOerQLB](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=S37hOerQLB); [arXiv:2510.16062](https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/2510.16062)). The cost is real: every claim verified is extra queries + a revise pass (SAFE-style fact-checking ran ~$0.49 and 28 min per long response in the fact-checking literature). The economically correct design is RARR's: **gate** — only verify claims the model is unsure of or that are check-worthy, only revise claims the evidence contradicts. *Agentic Design Patterns* (Ch.4 Reflection) grounds the structural rule: a **separate critic prevents the "cognitive bias" of an agent reviewing its own work**, and reliable reflection uses external tools/feedback rather than introspection — the same separation that made CoVe's independent verification and CRITIC's tool-feedback win.

## 5. Actionable implications for task-conditioned librarian experiments

1. **Make verify-revise a task-conditioned arm, not a default.** Coding/maths/single-fact lookups already have stronger verifiers (compiler, unit test, exact value) — there the loop should defer to *those*, not a librarian re-query (Huang et al.: external verifiers like code executors are where self-correction genuinely helps). Literature-synthesis and science-writing — long-form, many check-worthy claims, no executable oracle — are where librarian-grounded verify-revise pays off (CoVe/RARR territory). Hypothesis to test: net-correctness gain from the loop is positive only for the synthesis/writing tasks and ~zero or negative for lookup.
2. **Two separate post-hoc passes, scored separately.** (a) *claim verification* = re-query the librarian per check-worthy claim and revise on contradiction (RARR agreement gate); (b) *citation correction* = re-align each cited chunk by keyword+semantic match (CiteFix), not by asking an LLM. Report a claim-correction rate and a citation-correction rate independently; expect citation fixes to be cheap and high-yield (+15%) and claim fixes to be costlier.
3. **Issue verification queries independently of the draft.** Use Round-1's verbatim-query result here: phrase the verification search as a fresh factual query, never "is my sentence X supported?", to avoid CoVe's confirmation-bias failure. Measure attribution lift vs a draft-conditioned baseline.
4. **Cap iterations at 1-2 and gate per claim.** Benchmark a fixed 1-round gated loop against 2-3 ungated rounds; the literature predicts the gated single round wins on cost-adjusted quality (Self-Refine/CorrectBench diminishing returns). Track searches-per-task and revise-passes-per-task as first-class cost metrics.
5. **Adopt a four-way citation verdict (supported / partial / unsupported / uncertain).** Have the loop emit SemanticCite-style labels per cited chunk; "unsupported" triggers re-search-or-abstain (reusing the abstention contract as the revise action), "partial/uncertain" triggers a soften-the-claim edit. Measure how many "unverifiable" outputs were really just mis-cited (CiteFix found ~80%) — a large mis-citation share argues for cheap correction over expensive re-generation.
6. **Quality-gate the draft before looping.** Self-Refine: a structurally broken first draft drowns the critique signal. For the librarian, gate on whether the draft cited *any* retrieved chunk; if it cited none, re-retrieve rather than revise.
