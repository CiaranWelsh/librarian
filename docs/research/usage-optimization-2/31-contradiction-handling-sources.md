# Conflicting Sources During Synthesis: Detection, Prevalence, Resolution, and Presentation — and What It Means for Task-Conditioned Librarian Use

## Scope

When the librarian returns top-k chunks, it does not guarantee they agree. Two chunks from different textbooks, or a 2010 detector paper and a 2020 one, can support contradictory answers. This note collects the published evidence on (a) how *often* retrieved sets actually conflict, (b) how *badly* LLMs detect and handle that conflict, (c) which *resolution* strategies work, and (d) how to *present* conflict to the reader — then turns each into a task-conditioned usage experiment. This is the missing failure mode in Round 1's single-query mechanics: the abstention contract kills hallucination on *absent* evidence, but says nothing about *contradictory* evidence.

## Measured prevalence of conflict in retrieved sets

Conflict is the common case, not the edge case. The **RAMDocs** benchmark, built to mimic realistic retrieval, averages **5.53 documents per query of which 1.70 (≈31%) are misinformation or noise** and **2.20 valid answers per query** — i.e. genuine ambiguity plus contamination in almost every pool ([Wang et al. 2025, RAG with Conflicting Evidence](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.13079)). The **DRAGged into Conflicts** corpus, drawn from real top-10 Google results over five datasets, found only **161/458 (35%) of instances were conflict-free**; the rest split into complementary (115), conflicting opinions (115), outdated/freshness (62), and misinformation (5) ([Hagit et al. 2025, DRAGged into Conflicts](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.08500v2)). In scientific literature specifically — directly relevant to our particle-physics corpus — contradiction is "almost inevitable": **9 of 39 highly-cited clinical RCTs were later contradicted or found to have overestimated effects**, and contradiction-scoring across biomedical abstracts shows higher-contradiction bins rising from a minority pre-2000 to **roughly half of all documents in 2010–2025** ([Ioannidis-style review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1551714422001082); [biomedical contradiction trends, via search](https://insights.pluto.im/how-to-address-contradictory-findings-in-literature-reviews/)). Implication: a curated, version-stable book corpus has *less* freshness conflict than the open web, but textbooks still disagree on definitions, thresholds, and conventions — so conflict prevalence in the librarian is non-trivial and task-dependent (highest for synthesis over multiple books, near-zero for a single API lookup).

## Detection is hard — even for strong models

LLMs are poor, asymmetric conflict detectors. On a 1,867-sample synthetic context-validation set, the best config (Claude-3 Sonnet + CoT) reached only **71.0% accuracy / 0.71 F1**, with weaker models near random (38–40%); critically, all models showed **high precision but low recall — they miss real contradictions** ([Contradiction Detection as Context Validation 2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.00180)). Difficulty is type-graded: pair contradictions are detectable (**up to 89.3%**), but self-contradictions collapse to **0.006–0.456 accuracy**. Conflict-*type* classification is harder still — the best model in DRAGged into Conflicts predicted type at only **65.3%**, and ConflictRAG's four-class (factual/temporal/opinion/none) head reached **74.3%**, with **opinion conflicts hardest** and temporal easiest (F1 0.823) ([ConflictRAG 2026](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.17301v1)). Human inter-annotator agreement of only ~74% confirms the task is intrinsically hard, not just hard for models. Takeaway: silent merging is the default failure — a system that concatenates the 400 IU (2010) and 600–800 IU (2020) vitamin-D guidelines produces an inconsistent answer and never flags it.

## Resolution strategies and their measured payoff

Three approaches dominate, with quantified gains. (1) **Detect-classify-resolve-then-generate**: ConflictRAG's two-stage detector (cheap MLP + selective LLM) hits **90.8% detection accuracy / 88.7% F1 at 62% lower cost**, an Entropy-TOPSIS credibility weighting adds **7.1%** over manual heuristics, and the full pipeline gives **5.3–6.1% correctness** over the strongest conflict-aware baseline. (2) **Make the model reason about conflict type before answering**: in DRAGged into Conflicts, giving the gold conflict type lifted behavior-adherence by **~24 points** (e.g. GPT-4o 67.2%→88.6%); even *predicting* the type first (pipeline) added **~9 points**, and taxonomy-aware prompting **~5.5 points** — the hardest "conflicting opinions" class jumped 36.2%→73.3%. (3) **Multi-agent debate / aggregation**: Madam-RAG runs one agent per document and aggregates, gaining **11.4% (AmbigDocs) and 15.8% (FaithEval)** over Astute RAG — but RAMDocs accuracy stays at **34.4%**, so even the best resolver leaves most hard cases unsolved. A key warning: majority-weighting resolution **suppresses correct minority/dissenting views**, exactly the wrong behavior for frontier science.

## Presentation to readers

The consensus design principle is **surface conflict, do not collapse it** — especially under legitimate ambiguity where multiple answers are valid ([Wang et al. 2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.13079)). Practical mechanisms: hedging language calibrated to *actual* evidence disagreement (cite a range, attribute "some sources say X, others Y"); clickable/inspectable per-claim source links so readers verify; and explicit point-of-disagreement explanations that name the conflicting spans. Caveats matter: factuality/confidence indicators can mislead because **a response can be perfectly faithful to an unreliable source**, and **50–90% of LLM citations don't fully support their claims** — so a citation is necessary but not sufficient ([AI Transparency roadmap](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2306.01941); [citation support studies, via search](https://ziptie.dev/blog/how-llms-choose-sources-to-cite/)).

## Actionable implications for librarian usage experiments

1. **Add a conflict-detection arm to the abstention contract.** Round 1 gives support/refute/insufficient; extend to a fourth verdict — *conflicting* — judged over the accumulated chunk set. Measure recall (the documented weak spot), not just accuracy, since models "miss many real contradictions."

2. **Condition resolution effort on task.** Literature/science synthesis gets the full detect-classify-resolve pipeline and debate; a single coding/API or maths-definition lookup needs none (conflict prevalence ≈ 0). Test where the conflict-handling cost pays off per task type.

3. **Force conflict-type reasoning before answering on synthesis tasks.** The +24/+9-point DRAGged result is the cheapest large win available; prototype a "name the conflict type (factual/temporal/opinion), then answer" prompt and A/B it against verbatim generation.

4. **Treat freshness/temporal conflict as a librarian metadata problem.** Temporal conflicts are the easiest to classify (F1 0.823) and the breadcrumbs already carry source/edition — surface publication date in chunks and let the assistant prefer recent on freshness conflicts while flagging the older value.

5. **Default to "present, don't collapse," and ban silent majority-voting.** For physics-frontier queries, preserving a correct minority source matters more than a clean single answer. Score experiments on whether genuine multi-answer ambiguity is *reported* (RAMDocs-style multi-answer EM), not just whether one answer is right.

6. **Quantify the librarian's conflict rate per task as a first experiment.** Before tuning resolution, measure baseline: over a task-stratified query sample, how often do the top-k chunks actually disagree? This is the analogue of Round 1's k-curve and sets the budget for everything above.
